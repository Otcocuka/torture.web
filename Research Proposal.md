Cross-Cultural Validation of an AI-Driven Cognitive Learning System: A Proposed Study for MEXT/G30 Master's Program

1. Introduction and Background

The integration of artificial intelligence into educational technology has created unprecedented opportunities for personalized learning. Large Language Models (LLMs) enable semantic analysis of educational content and generation of adaptive assessments at scale. However, most existing research focuses on Western educational contexts, leaving a significant gap in understanding how AI-driven learning systems perform across different cultural and educational environments.

This proposal outlines a planned Master's research project that builds upon a fully functional prototype — an intelligent system integrating personal productivity tools (Pomodoro, Kanban, habit tracking) with a cognitive learning engine featuring LLM-powered knowledge extraction, adaptive quizzes, and spaced repetition. The prototype was developed as a Bachelor's thesis project and has demonstrated promising results through simulation modeling.

2. Research Questions

RQ1: How does the effectiveness of an AI-driven cognitive learning system (measured by retention rate, cognitive load, and learning efficiency) vary between Japanese and Russian university students?

RQ2: Can biometric data (EEG, eye-tracking) serve as reliable indicators of cognitive load and engagement during AI-assisted learning sessions?

RQ3: What cultural factors influence the adoption and effectiveness of integrated productivity-learning systems?

3. Methodology

3.1. Study Design. A mixed-methods cross-cultural comparative study with the following components:

Quantitative component: Randomized controlled trial (RCT) with n=60 participants per cohort (Japan, Russia), 8-week duration. Pre/post testing with standardized assessments, NASA-TLX cognitive load measurement, and System Usability Scale (SUS).
Qualitative component: Semi-structured interviews, think-aloud protocols during system usage, and cultural adaptation analysis.
Biometric component: EEG recording during learning sessions (frontal theta power as indicator of cognitive engagement), eye-tracking for attention distribution analysis.
3.2. System Enhancement. The existing prototype will be extended with:

Biometric data integration module (EEG via OpenBCI, eye-tracking via webcam-based solutions)
Cross-cultural interface adaptation (Japanese language support, cultural UI/UX adjustments)
Enhanced analytics dashboard with real-time cognitive load visualization
3.3. Data Analysis. Statistical analysis will include:

Mixed-effects ANOVA for cross-cultural comparison
Structural equation modeling (SEM) for factor analysis
Machine learning models for predicting learning outcomes from biometric and behavioral data 4. Expected Outcomes

Empirical evidence of the system's cross-cultural effectiveness, contributing to the generalizability of AI-driven educational interventions.
A validated framework for integrating biometric feedback into adaptive learning systems.
Open-source dataset of learning interactions with biometric annotations for the research community.
Publications in IEICE, Journal of Educational Technology & Society, or similar Scopus-indexed journals. 5. Significance for MEXT/G30 Program

This research directly aligns with the MEXT objective of fostering international academic collaboration. The cross-cultural design requires deep integration with Japanese academic institutions and researchers, creating lasting research partnerships. The biometric component leverages Japan's strengths in human-computer interaction research. The project combines computer science, cognitive psychology, and educational technology — inherently interdisciplinary fields that benefit from the G30 program's international environment.

The Bachelor's prototype includes: LLM-powered knowledge extraction with topic clustering, step-based spaced repetition algorithm with intervals [1, 3, 7, 14, 28] days, adaptive quiz engine with continuous scoring (0–100%), and structured research data pipeline. Simulation analysis on open educational data (Student Performance dataset, n=649) suggests potential improvements of 15–25% in learning retention and 18–23% reduction in cognitive load indicators.

The complete source code, thesis documentation, sample research data, and simulation scripts are publicly available at https://github.com/Otcocuka/torture.web under MIT license.
